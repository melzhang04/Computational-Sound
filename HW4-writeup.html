For this project, I aimed to recreate the sound of distant, rolling thunder based on Farnell’s Designing Sound, Practical 17. Rather than trying to imitate a single sharp lightning crack, I focused on the more layered version of thunder that unfolds over time. Farnell describes thunder as a combination of multiple separate components rather than one single sound, and that idea shaped my entire design.

Synthesis and Code Walkthrough

My thunder sound is made from four layers mixed together in strikeThunder(): a strike layer, a rumble layer, an afterimage layer, and a deep bass layer. Each one is routed into its own gain node, then combined into a pre-master bus, panned, scaled by a final master gain, and sent to the speakers. The code also adds a separate echo network based on multiple filtered delay taps to create a sense of outdoor space.

The first layer is the strike. I used short bursts of white noise passed through two bandpass filters, with brighter and shorter bursts for stronger strikes and duller, longer bursts for weaker ones. The strike pattern is created by increasing a time accumulator so that each later burst happens farther apart, which follows Farnell’s idea that multistrike lightning becomes less energetic and less frequent over time.

The second layer is the rumble, which gives the thunder its long rolling texture. I used two brown-noise sources, heavy low-pass filtering, waveshaping, and additional bandpass and low-pass filtering to create a softer, moving low-frequency body. This section was mainly noise synthesis with subtractive filtering, with waveshaping added to make the texture more complex.

The third layer is the afterimage, which acts like reflected mid-range clutter from buildings or trees. In my code, one noise source modulates another through a clipped gate, and the result is filtered around the midrange. I chose this because thunder reflections should not sound clean; they should feel scattered and uneven.

The fourth layer is the deep noise tail. For this, I used brown noise, low-pass filters, a high-pass filter to remove unusable subsonic energy, and soft clipping to add harmonics before smoothing the signal again. This layer starts later than the initial strike and rises more slowly, which helps create the feeling of a dramatic bass swell at the end of the sound.

Finally, I added environmental echoes using multiple delay taps with different filter settings and stereo positions. Shorter delays are brighter, while longer delays are darker and softer. This helped the sound feel less dry and more like it was happening across a large outdoor space.

Process and Experience

My process was mostly iterative. I began with the strike, then added the rumble, then reflections, and finally the deep bass and echo layers. The hardest part was balancing realism with clarity. If I made the rumble too noisy, it sounded windy or static-like instead of like thunder. If I made the strike too sharp, it sounded more like an explosion or a click than a thunder event. I had to keep adjusting filter ranges, gain levels, and envelope timings so that each layer contributed something different without overwhelming the others. The slider controls in the interface also helped me test close, mid, far, and rolling thunder variations more quickly.

Overall, this project helped me understand how a complex environmental sound can be built from a combination of simpler synthesis methods.
